# TX-11: August Pfluger — Financial Disclosure Summary

Rep. August Pfluger (R-TX11) raises money through three committees: his principal campaign committee, **August Pfluger for Congress** (C00719294); a joint fundraising committee, **Pfluger Victory Committee** (C00753913), which splits large checks among Pfluger's campaign and allied committees; and a mostly dormant leadership PAC, **PFriends of Pfluger** (C00840033). Across itemized FEC filings covering January 2025 through mid-2026, the three committees together raised **$4.24 million** and spent **$5.80 million** in itemized, non-memo transactions. The gap isn't a fundraising problem — it reflects a large cash cushion carried into the cycle: the principal campaign committee began the period with $2.32 million on hand and still grew it to $2.88 million, while the Victory Committee's own cash balance climbed from $64,727 to $293,100.

"Transfers" is the joint fundraising committee doing its job — routing pooled money to Pfluger's own campaign ($1.69 million), Raptor PAC ($720,323), and the House Conservatives Fund ($70,803), on top of direct contributions to the NRCC ($863,351 combined across transfers and political-contribution line items). Within Administrative/Salary/Overhead, the two largest named payees are staffers Caroline Brennan ($148,627) and Grace Dunham ($124,685), alongside compliance and consulting firms CFS Compliance ($95,037), payroll platform Gusto ($85,227), and Austin-based Norfleet Strategies ($58,067). Targeted Victory ($342,925) and Targeted Creative Communications ($46,318) handle advertising; Ragnar Research Partners ($34,800) handles polling.

The next-biggest single vendors are American Express ($652,300) and Citi ($169,791) — both lump card payments, but not the dead end that might look like. FEC's own memo-item linkage breaks 120 such lump payments, totaling $973,867, down into 1,733 real, vendor-level sub-transactions totaling $908,713 — 93.3% of the lump total itemized at the merchant level. That layer shows $260,398 in travel (American Airlines alone: $71,541) and $332,714 in fundraising-related spend, including a run of upscale Washington, DC dining — The Capital Grille ($34,006), Del Frisco's Double Eagle Steakhouse ($27,582), Oceanaire ($19,977), Fiola Mare ($10,420), and Tosca ($11,197) — plus the Capitol Hill Club ($12,850), the members-only Royal Sands Social Club ($15,113 across three catering/facility-rental payments), and premium car services Savoya and BLS Limo Group ($24,272 combined).

Two payments stand outside the card system entirely, both booked through the Victory Committee for donor-facing events: **Stein Erikson Lodge**, a five-star ski resort in Park City, UT ($43,959, "facility rental and catering," August 2025), and **St. Joe Resort and Operations LLC** in Panama City Beach, FL ($37,819, "facility rental & catering," October 2025). And in a smaller but oddly specific line item, the campaign spent $10,542 across ten transactions at a single Macy's department store in Fair Oaks, VA over two days in early January 2025, all coded "SUPPORTER GIFTS."

## Takeaways

1. **One donor, $642,100, one mechanism.** Syed Javaid Anwar of Midland Energy is the single largest funding relationship in the dataset — roughly 15% of all itemized money raised — made possible entirely through the joint fundraising committee, which lets one donor's combined checks exceed what any single participating committee could accept on its own.

2. **A retirement account pivoted straight into the donor base's industry.** Pfluger's House Ethics stock-trade filings show his own Roth IRA selling off a diversified slate of media and entertainment holdings piece by piece through 2025 and into early 2026 — Liberty Media, SiriusXM, Walt Disney, Warner Bros. Discovery (twice), Fidelity National Financial — each sale in the modest $1,001–$15,000 band. Then, on March 13, 2026, that same account made six brand-new purchases, each in the larger $15,001–$50,000 band: Dorchester Minerals, Enterprise Products Partners, and Kimbell Royalty Partners (all oil-and-gas royalty or midstream plays), Viper Energy, plus Berkshire Hathaway and Amerco (U-Haul's parent). The rotation — smaller media-stock sales funding larger energy-sector buys — landed in the same fundraising cycle in which Permian Basin oil and gas executives were Pfluger's single largest donor bloc. Separate accounts held in trust for his spouse and a dependent child (via a corporate spinoff and a merger, respectively) still hold their own untouched media-stock positions and weren't part of this rotation.

3. **More than half of "spending" never touches a voter.** The $3.17 million "Transfers" category — 55% of all itemized disbursements — is money moving between Pfluger-aligned committees (his own campaign, Raptor PAC, House Conservatives Fund, NRCC), not ads or field organizing. Anyone citing a "spending" total without separating this out will overstate direct campaign activity.

4. **Donor hospitality shows up at both ends of the price scale.** The Victory Committee paid a Park City ski resort and a Florida Panhandle beach resort nearly $82,000 combined for "facility rental and catering," plus recurring tabs at a members-only DC social club. At the other extreme, the itemized filings show a very specific $10,542 spent at one Macy's store in Fair Oaks, VA over two days in January 2025 for "supporter gifts" — the kind of retail-receipt-level detail itemized FEC disclosure surfaces that a press release never would.

5. **The card-processor spending isn't actually a black box.** It would be easy to write off the $652,300 American Express and $169,791 Citi totals as opaque lump sums. But FEC filings link both to thousands of memo-coded sub-transactions with real merchant names, and reading those instead of stopping at the card-issuer line turns up the DC dining and travel pattern above — none of it visible if you stop at the AmEx line item.

## Methodology & AI Transparency

This summary was produced by **Claude Sonnet 5** (model ID `claude-sonnet-5`), running as an agentic coding assistant inside the Claude Code VS Code extension. The session used the harness's default sampling configuration — no explicit temperature or max-output-token override was set by the operator, and those values are controlled by the Claude Code runtime rather than exposed to the model.

Analysis was tool-augmented, not a single prompt-to-text completion. Rather than writing new tooling, the model reused the existing [`/tooling/analyze-candidate.rb`](../../tooling/analyze-candidate.rb) (Ruby 3.3.8, dependencies managed via Bundler/`Gemfile`), invoked as:

```
bundle exec ruby analyze-candidate.rb --fec-dir tx-11/august-pfluger/fec --house-ethics-dir tx-11/august-pfluger/house-ethics
```

That script parses FEC Schedule A (itemized receipts) and Schedule B (itemized disbursements) CSVs for every committee directory it finds, excluding FEC memo-only entries from Schedule A (redundant conduit/earmark rollups) while preserving Schedule B memo rows separately as merchant-level detail behind lump card payments, netting negative correction rows (chargebacks, refunds) into running totals rather than discarding them, and restricting the donor view to `line_number_label`s that represent an actual outside contribution rather than inter-committee transfers or bank interest. See the strategy comments at the top of the script for the full rationale behind each of these choices.

Per this prompt's instruction to verify the tool's documented data-integrity gotchas against this specific candidate's data before trusting any total — not assume they're moot just because they're already coded — two checks were run by hand on the raw CSVs, independent of the tool's own summary output:

- **Duplicate/amended `transaction_id` values.** Every schedule file in this candidate's data has unique transaction IDs, with one exception: two `transaction_id`s in the Victory Committee's Schedule A (contributions from Robert LeGrand and Ben Stribling) each appear twice, as a memo/non-memo pair rather than a competing amendment. The tool's donor-totals logic already excludes memo rows from Schedule A, so these are counted once, not twice — no fix was needed, but it's exactly the pattern the script's header comments warn a future candidate's data might not follow, so it's worth re-checking each time rather than assuming.
- **The House Ethics PDFs, read directly rather than through the tool's dollar-amount grep.** `analyze-candidate.rb` extracts PDF text and surfaces lines containing a dollar figure — a starting point for a human to read, not a structured parse, and its own header comments say so. Column-based PDF layouts extract as run-together text (e.g., an owner code and an asset name merging into one garbled line), which is unreliable for a claim as specific as "who owns this position" or "was this a purchase or a sale." All eight House Ethics filings in this candidate's directory were therefore read directly, page by page, rather than trusted from the grep output. That direct read is what distinguished self-owned Roth IRA activity (no owner code) from a spouse's RBC Roth IRA (owner code "SP") and a dependent child's CP Roth IRA (owner code "DC") in Takeaway #2 above, and confirmed transaction types (Purchase/Sale/Exchange) that the grep-only pass could not reliably surface. One of the eight PDFs (Filing #30026980) is not a trade disclosure at all — it's a 90-day extension request pushing the annual Financial Disclosure covering 2025 to an August 13, 2026 due date, meaning the full annual asset/income snapshot isn't in this dataset yet; only the incremental Periodic Transaction Reports are.

Neither check changed a headline dollar figure versus what the tool's own summary output showed. The PDF re-read did change the framing of a finding, though: reading the source documents directly turned a vague "personal stock purchases in the same window as donor courting" into a specific, verifiable claim about which account made which trade, in what size, and how that compares to the family's separately held positions. The FEC cash-summary figures in the opening paragraph were read directly from screenshots as images. Readers should independently verify any figure before republishing; FEC data is self-reported by campaigns and subject to amendment.
