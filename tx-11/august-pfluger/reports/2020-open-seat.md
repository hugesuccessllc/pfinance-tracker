# TX-11: August Pfluger — Financial Disclosure Summary (2020 Cycle)

Per `historical-election-results.md`, 2020 was the cycle where TX-11 actually had a contest: Mike Conaway's retirement opened the seat, and Pfluger won a ten-way Republican primary field with 56,093 of 107,435 votes cast before beating Democrat Jon Mark Hogg (53,394 votes) and Libertarian Wacey Alpha Cody (5,811 votes) in the general, 232,568 to 53,394 to 5,811. This is the only one of the three cycles in this report set where Pfluger had to win a real primary, and the financial data reads differently as a result — spending is front-loaded, media-heavy, and largely happens before either the campaign committee or Raptor PAC (newly formed for this cycle) had built the fundraising machinery visible in later years. All three locally collected committees are used: the principal campaign committee (**August Pfluger for Congress**, C00719294), the leadership PAC (**Raptor PAC**, C00749481), and the joint fundraising committee (**Pfluger Victory Fund**, C00753913) — though the JFC barely registers this cycle, since it didn't yet carry the fundraising load it would in 2022 and beyond.

Combined itemized donor receipts across the three committees total **$2,532,145** for the 2020 cycle — the smallest of the three cycles examined, and the only one where the campaign committee itself ($2.25M) raised far more than the JFC ($47K). 87% of receipts were from individuals ($2.18M) versus 13% from PACs/committees ($347K). Combined disbursements were **$2,191,454** — with no large inter-committee transfer pattern distorting the total, unlike the later, JFC-dominated cycles.

## Major Spending

The category breakdown for 2020 looks structurally different from 2022/2024: there's no dominant "Transfers" line, because neither the JFC nor the fundraising machine were yet built out.

- **Uncategorized — $1.32M** (310 items, the largest category by far). This is a data-quality artifact rather than a genuine spending category: FEC's `disbursement_purpose_category` field was inconsistently populated in this earlier cycle's filings. Treat the vendor-level breakdown below as the more reliable read than the category label.
- **Administrative/salary/overhead — $313K** (100 items).
- **Advertising — $261K** (4 items only — a small number of very large media buys).
- **Political contributions — $143K** (116 items).
- **Solicitation/fundraising — $91K** (19 items).
- **Travel — just $736** (1 item) — a striking contrast with $174K (2024) and $51K (2022) in later cycles, consistent with a state-based primary/general candidate who hadn't yet taken office and had no Washington travel pattern to fund.

The top vendor by far is **Flexpoint Media Inc. ($579,757)**, a media-buying firm, most of it landing in the "Uncategorized" bucket despite clearly being advertising placement (line-item descriptions read "PLACED MEDIA"). Close behind: **Norfleet Strategies ($351,778)**, a recurring vendor also seen in the 2022 and 2024 reports; **Upstream Communications ($205,154)**; and **Ragnar Research Partners ($41,500)** — a polling/research firm, consistent with a candidate actually needing to test messaging in a real ten-way primary, unlike the polling anomaly flagged in the unopposed 2022 cycle.

The largest single disbursements are almost entirely Flexpoint Media placements clustered in January–February and September 2020 ($232,380; $136,233; $89,650; $71,317; $50,177) — a media strategy weighted toward the primary period (Jan–Feb, ahead of the March 2020 primary) with a second wave ahead of the November general.

## Takeaways

1. **A real primary produced real campaign spending — the opposite pattern from 2022 and 2024.** Nearly $1.06M in identifiable media/advertising/consulting spending (Flexpoint Media, Norfleet Strategies, Upstream Communications combined) went out in a cycle where Pfluger actually needed to win votes, compared to the fundraising-and-influence-heavy spending seen once he was a safe incumbent. The contrast across these three reports is itself the finding.

2. **Money flowed to the man whose seat he was trying to fill.** The transfer-recipient data shows a **$2,500** contribution from Pfluger's committee to **Conaway, Michael [C00383828]** — the outgoing incumbent whose retirement created the open seat. A newcomer's committee writing a check to the very member he's succeeding reads as a relationship/endorsement gesture rather than ordinary spending, and is flagged below as worth a specific follow-up look.

3. **A possible family contribution.** Karen Pfluger, retired, San Angelo — the candidate's hometown — gave the maximum $10,600 split across the campaign and Raptor PAC. Combined with the near-total absence of PAC money in the top-10 donor list (a sharp contrast with 2022 and 2024), this cycle's donor base reads as personal-network fundraising for a first-time candidate rather than the institutional-donor network built up in later cycles.

4. **A data-quality gap, not a spending mystery.** $1.32M — 60% of this cycle's itemized disbursements — falls under "Uncategorized" in the FEC's own purpose-category field. The vendor-level detail (Flexpoint Media, Norfleet Strategies, Upstream Communications) makes clear this is overwhelmingly media and consulting spend, not an actual black box, but it's a reminder that category-based totals from this specific cycle's filings are less reliable than in 2022/2024 and should be read at the vendor level.

5. **Near-zero travel spending marks the "not yet in Congress" period.** At $736 total, travel spending in 2020 is two orders of magnitude smaller than in the two cycles that followed — the clearest single marker in this dataset of the shift from state-based candidate to sitting member of Congress.
