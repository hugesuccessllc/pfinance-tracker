# TX-11: August Pfluger — Oil & Gas Money in the 2026 Receipts

*A look at exactly which of August Pfluger's 2026-cycle donors — people and companies — trace to the oil and gas industry, with a receipt for every dollar.*

## The short version

Of the **$5,239,920.63** in itemized donor receipts recorded across August Pfluger's three 2026-cycle committees, **$1,305,669.21** — very nearly **one dollar in every four (24.9%)** — comes from donors this report can independently tie to the oil and gas industry: company executives, landmen, mineral-rights owners, oilfield-service operators, and the PACs of drillers, refiners, and pipeline companies. That figure is deliberately conservative — built from a keyword scan, then manually checked line by line, with a dozen ambiguous or wrong matches thrown out before anything was counted (see "What counted, and what didn't," below).

This isn't a surprise for a candidate who represents West Texas and sits on the Energy and Commerce Committee — Pfluger's disclosures already signal that the Permian Basin is the foundation of his fundraising. What this report adds is the receipt-level detail: which companies, which executives, how much, and where to verify it yourself.

## One donor, one-in-two dollars

**Syed Javaid Anwar** — president and CEO of Midland-based **Midland Energy, Inc.** — gave **$642,100** to the Pfluger Victory Committee across two checks: $332,100 on 2025-02-07 and $310,000 on 2026-03-16 (`fec/C00753913/schedule_a-2026-07-20T20_08_10.csv:3004`, `:3003`). That's **49.2%** of every oil-and-gas dollar counted in this report, from a single donor. Anwar has previously been reported as a major backer of Governor Greg Abbott and a Texas Higher Education Coordinating Board appointee — public profile this report doesn't re-verify beyond noting it's consistent with what the FEC filing itself says about his employer and title.

The next two largest checks are $50,000 each, both landing the same week in February 2025: **William Crump**, an engineer at **BC Operating, Inc.** (`fec/C00753913/schedule_a-2026-07-20T20_08_10.csv:3001`), and **Cody Campbell**, co-CEO of **Double Eagle Energy** (`:3000`). Both are already-familiar names from Pfluger's broader donor profile — this report just confirms the industry link directly from their own filed occupation/employer fields, rather than inferring it.

## One committee carries almost all of it

Of the $1,305,669.21 total, **$1,268,350.00 (97.1%)** flowed through the **Pfluger Victory Committee** — the joint fundraising committee (JFC) that pools money for Pfluger and his allies — versus just **$37,319.21** given directly to his principal campaign committee, and **nothing** identifiably oil-and-gas-connected to **Raptor PAC**, his leadership PAC. Within the JFC alone, oil-and-gas money is **27.9%** of its entire $4,549,850.00 in itemized 2026 receipts.

That lopsidedness matches a pattern this project has already documented for Pfluger: a JFC can legally accept much larger checks than a single campaign committee (the per-donor limit is the sum of all the participating committees' own limits), which is exactly the mechanism that let a $642,100 single-donor check happen at all. Anyone who only pulled the principal committee's report — the $37,319.21 slice — would see almost none of this.

**$110,781.00** of the total (25 of the 205 matched rows) comes from raw `efile-*.csv` submissions dated after the JFC and principal committee's processed `schedule_a` exports stop (2026-03-31) — donations as recent as May 2026 that the FEC's own processed data doesn't yet reflect. See Methodology below for how this was pulled in without double-counting.

## What counted, and what didn't

This report started from an automated keyword scan (`tooling/donor-keyword-scan.rb`, written for this report) against every donor's name, employer, and occupation, then every one of the 250 raw matches was checked by hand — reading the row, and where the connection wasn't obvious, searching the company's own name to confirm what it actually does. That check found real problems worth naming, because they're exactly the kind of mistake a purely automated version of this report would have shipped:

- **Surname coincidences, not industry connections ($18,063.06 excluded).** "Gas" and "oil" are substrings of ordinary surnames — Roland **Dugas**, Robert **Gaston**, Luda **Gashenko**, Ken **Gaspar**, and both Jason and Charlotte **Pendergast** (whose actual employers are a tax-prep firm and an aggregates company) all matched on nothing but their last names. None has any filed connection to energy.
- **A different kind of "exploration" ($5,000 excluded).** The Space Exploration Technologies Corp. PAC — SpaceX — matched on the keyword "exploration." It has nothing to do with oil and gas.
- **Real energy companies that aren't oil and gas ($48,800 excluded).** Douglass Robison's **Natura Resources** is an advanced-nuclear company, not oil and gas, despite the "Resources" match (already flagged in this project's notes on the Robison family — see the household's other filings for the nuclear detail). **Maas Energy Works** ($6,800, three checks) turns out to be a California renewable-natural-gas company that converts dairy manure into biogas — genuinely a different business than fossil-fuel production. **OCIS Intelligent Energy** ($3,500) builds natural-gas-and-battery power infrastructure for data centers, closer to the AI power-buildout story than to a drilling company. And six utility/nuclear employee PACs — Sempra, NextEra, the Nuclear Energy Institute, Xcel, Holtec, PG&E, and Constellation — all matched on "energy" but represent regulated electric utilities or nuclear technology firms, not oil-and-gas producers.
- **One plausible match left unconfirmed and excluded ($1,000).** Randal J. Gardner's employer, "Cirrus Resources," could not be confirmed as any of the several similarly-named Texas oil-and-gas companies found in a search — none turned up at the San Angelo address on file. Rather than guess, it's left out of the total.
- **One initially-ambiguous match, corroborated and kept ($12,000).** Tim G. Culp's employer, "Double T Energy & Investments" (Midland), doesn't turn up under that exact name in a company search — but "Double T" is a well-established family of Midland oilfield-service businesses (Double T Oilfield Services rents equipment to Permian operators), and the Midland address plus "Energy" in the name make the connection strong enough to include.

The scoping also follows the rules this project already uses to avoid double-counting a candidate's own money: only receipt rows the FEC's own `line_number_label` field marks as **"Contributions From Individuals"** or **"Contributions From Other Political Committees"** count as a donor giving money — never a "Transfers from authorized committees" row, which is how a JFC's own pooled proceeds move to a participating committee (and which, per this project's existing data-integrity notes, separately carries earmark-attribution rows that would double-count against the JFC's own direct receipts if summed here too).

## What this actually says

1. **A quarter of every dollar Pfluger's committees have taken in during the 2026 cycle traces to oil and gas** — not as a loose industry vibe, but as a specific, line-cited list of executives, landmen, mineral owners, and company PACs.
2. **It's concentrated, not diffuse.** One donor (Anwar) is very nearly half of it; the next handful of names covers most of the rest. This isn't hundreds of small checks that happen to add up — it's a compact circle of Permian Basin operators who mostly already know each other.
3. **The JFC is the mechanism, not a coincidence.** 97% of the oil-and-gas money went through the joint fundraising committee that can legally accept far larger checks than the campaign itself — the same structural point this project has made about Pfluger's fundraising generally, just now isolated to a single industry.
4. **Geography tracks the district, mostly.** Midland and Odessa — the heart of the Permian Basin and part of TX-11 — dominate the donor list, which is exactly what you'd expect from a member representing that district. The exceptions (Transatlantic Petroleum's Fort Worth-based founder, an Albuquerque operator, a Houston power-infrastructure company that didn't make the final cut) are the interesting outliers precisely because they aren't local.
5. **None of this is unusual or hidden.** Every donor and dollar here is exactly what the candidate reported to the FEC. The finding isn't that the money exists — it's how much of it there is, once someone actually adds it up.

## A few honest caveats

This is a keyword-and-manual-review exercise, not an industry classification handed down by the FEC — there is no "oil and gas" checkbox on a Schedule A filing. A donor's occupation or employer field is free text supplied by the filer, and it can be stale, misspelled, or simply absent (several rows in the raw efile-gap data had blank employer/occupation fields and were only matched because the contributor's own business name — e.g. "James Wilkes Oil & Gas" — carried the keyword). A differently-worded or unlisted company name would not appear here even if it belongs. Every dollar figure above was checked against the source CSV at the cited file and line before publication; readers are encouraged to open those files and check the work themselves. And as always: legal, fully disclosed campaign contributions are not evidence of anything improper by themselves — this report counts and traces the money, and leaves judgment about what it means to the reader.
