# TX-11: August Pfluger — Campaign Spending as a Portrait of the Donor Class

*A look at the fine dining, ski-town retreats, chauffeured cars, and donor swag paid for by August Pfluger's campaign committees, and what it says about who his political operation actually serves.*

## The short version

Between roughly December 2024 and June 2026, August Pfluger's three campaign committees — **August Pfluger for Congress** (his principal committee), **Raptor PAC** (his leadership PAC), and the **Pfluger Victory Committee** (a joint fundraising committee, or JFC, that pools money for Pfluger and his allies before splitting it back out) — spent about **$678,000** on steakhouses, private clubs, ski-resort lodges, golf-club rentals, chauffeured black cars, and gifts for donors. That's out of roughly **$8.1 million** in total spending across the three committees during the period covered by the data — call it **1 dollar in every 12**.

A quick note on that date range: most of this report draws on the FEC's "processed" disbursement exports, but those lag the campaign's own raw electronic filings by weeks or months — as of this writing, the principal committee's and JFC's processed exports stopped at the end of March 2026, while their raw filings already covered spending through the end of June 2026. This report pulls in that extra quarter where it's safe to do so (see Methodology below for how double-counting was avoided), which is why some of the figures below include a handful of charges as recent as June 2026.

None of this is illegal. It's also, almost entirely, not paid for out of Pfluger's own pocket — it's paid for with money donors gave the campaign, spent largely on *courting more donors*. That loop — oil money in, steakhouse dinners and ski weekends for oil money out — is the actual story here. A joint fundraising committee, for readers who haven't run into the term before, is a shared bank account that multiple campaigns/PACs set up together to split the cost and hassle of raising money, then divides the proceeds among the participants according to a pre-agreed formula.

## Who's paying for the filet mignon

Before getting to what the money bought, it's worth a quick word on where it came from, because the two halves tell one story. Pfluger represents TX-11 — West Texas, anchored by the Permian Basin oil patch — and his top donors read like a directory of Permian operators. The single largest identifiable donor across his committees, at **$642,100**, is Syed Javaid Anwar, president/CEO of Midland-based **Midland Energy, Inc.** — a Pakistani immigrant turned Permian Basin oil titan who has also poured millions into Governor Greg Abbott's campaigns and sits on the Texas Higher Education Coordinating Board by Abbott's appointment ([E&E News](https://www.eenews.net/articles/major-texas-oil-donor-emerges-this-cycle-who-is-he/); [Bloomberg Government](https://news.bgov.com/bloomberg-government-news/oil-titans-hildebrand-anwar-break-for-trump-in-cash-race)). Right behind him: William Crump and Cody Campbell, both energy executives, at $50,000 each; a cluster of Midland and Odessa oil-and-gas families (Sellers, Robison, Strickling, Angelo, Bates) giving $12,000–$25,000 apiece; and PAC checks from Koch Industries, Valero, Chevron-adjacent Chenier Energy, and the American Petroleum Institute. Individual and PAC gifts totaled roughly $4.3 million across the committees in this window.

That's the raw material. What follows is where a meaningful slice of it went back out the door.

## Fine dining: a $265,000 tab, mostly six blocks from the Capitol

Pfluger's committees spent **$264,658.12** across 174 separate restaurant charges in this window — and the geography tells you what kind of "campaigning" this is. All but a handful of these charges are in Washington, DC, within easy walking distance of the Capitol. This isn't rubber-chicken constituent town halls; it's the DC fundraising circuit, where a member of Congress buys a lobbyist or a PAC director a steak in exchange for a check.

**The Capital Grille** — the white-tablecloth steakhouse chain, not a diner — is the single most-visited spot, appearing **69 times for $49,430.75** total (`fec/C00719294/schedule_b-2026-07-19T17_24_58.csv:2214`, `:2148`, `:2124`, and dozens more; `fec/C00749481/schedule_b-2026-07-19T17_30_43.csv:431`; one additional April 2026 charge from the raw efile at `fec/C00719294/efile-2026-07-19T17_25_08.csv:765`). Individual visits range from a $63.80 solo lunch up to a **$3,570.35** group dinner on 2024-11-21 (`C00719294/schedule_b:2215`) — the kind of tab that means a table, not a to-go order.

**Oceanaire Seafood Room**, a DC seafood house, shows up 21 times for **$38,878.44**, including a **$7,849.14** single charge on 2026-01-22 booked through the Pfluger Victory Committee (`fec/C00753913/schedule_b-2026-07-19T17_27_00.csv:318`), a **$4,410.72** charge on 2025-06-05 (`C00719294/schedule_b:2286`), and a **$1,475.41** charge on 2026-06-08 (`C00719294/efile-2026-07-19T17_25_08.csv:444`) — one of the most recent charges in the entire dataset. **Del Frisco's Double Eagle Steakhouse** — another DC power-dinner staple — appears only 3 times but for **$32,182.00**, headlined by a single **$27,582.00** charge on 2025-01-19, days after Pfluger was sworn in for the new Congress (`C00719294/schedule_b:2404`).

Then there's the private-club circuit. The **Capitol Hill Club** — a Republicans-only private club a block from the Capitol, membership by sponsorship only, with reduced dues specifically carved out for members of Congress ([Capitol Hill Club](https://www.capitolhillclub.org/Membership)) — was billed **$21,156.92** across 16 charges, including back-to-back $5,000-plus dinners on 2025-05-12 and 2025-05-15 (`C00719294/schedule_b:2328`, `:2317`) and three more visits in April and June 2026 (`C00719294/efile:569`, `:640`, `:493`) that hadn't yet shown up in the FEC's processed export as of this report. The **Royal Sands Social Club**, a members-only DC lounge, took **$15,112.96** across just 3 charges (`C00719294/schedule_b:2308`, `:2304`, `:2293`). Waterfront Italian spot **Fiola Mare** — a Georgetown fixture with harbor views — picked up **$10,420.04** over 10 visits (`C00719294/schedule_b:2282`, `:2176`, and others).

Rounding out the circuit: **Mi Vida**, a Mexican fine-dining spot on the DC waterfront, billed **$17,158.55** across just 4 charges — including two identical **$4,235.5x** charges eleven days apart in April 2026 (`C00753913/efile:112`, `:118`), both from the raw efile, not yet in the processed export. **AMA Restaurant** took **$16,689.47** across 13 visits; **Ocean Prime** billed $9,717.01 over 8 visits; **Trattoria Alberto** billed $8,771.62 over 4 visits; and smaller tabs went to **Tosca**, **Jackie American Bistro**, **Mastro's Steakhouse** (both the DC and Houston locations), and **Old Ebbitt Grill** — DC's oldest saloon, a five-minute walk from the White House. The overwhelming majority of these charges are filed under the FEC's "Solicitation and Fundraising Expenses" or "Campaign Event Expenses" categories, meaning the campaign itself is telling the FEC these meals are for raising money, not running the office.

## Retreats and resorts: courting donors in ski boots

If the DC steakhouse circuit is where Pfluger's operation entertains lobbyists between votes, a **$242,557.90** tab across 130 charges shows where it entertains bigger donors when Congress isn't in session: ski country.

The standout is **Stein Eriksen Lodge**, a five-star ski-in/ski-out resort at Deer Valley in Park City, Utah, where rooms alone commonly run $500–$900 a night and suites can top $2,900 ([Kayak](https://www.kayak.com/Park-City-Hotels-Stein-Eriksen-Lodge.36100.ksp); [Booking.com](https://www.booking.com/hotel/us/steineriksenlodgedeervalley.html)). The Pfluger Victory Committee paid the lodge **$43,959.03** in a single "facility rental and catering" charge on 2025-08-19 (`fec/C00753913/schedule_b-2026-07-19T17_27_00.csv:338`) — a single line item large enough that it also shows up in the principal committee's own top-40 disbursements list. Across all four Stein Eriksen/Eriksen-spelled charges, the total is **$48,181.82**.

That Park City weekend wasn't an isolated booking — it's part of a cluster. The same donor trip (or a closely timed one) also shows charges at the **St. Regis Deer Valley Resort** ($11,174.44 on 2025-10-09, `C00753913/schedule_b:324`), the **Montage Deer Valley**, and plain "Deer Valley Resort," totaling **$22,158.59** across 11 charges. Add the broader **St. Regis** brand (Washington, DC and Deer Valley combined) at **$22,493.92** across 9 charges, and **Ritz-Carlton** properties in Dallas, Key Biscayne, and Pentagon City at **$13,810.99** across 19 charges, and the pattern is a rotating circuit of five-star hospitality brands, not a budget conference room.

The same day the Stein Eriksen charge posted — 2025-08-19 — the Pfluger Victory Committee also paid **$10,297.22** to **Baltusrol Golf Club** in Springfield, New Jersey, for "facility rental" (`C00753913/schedule_b:323`), plus a second $2,257.33 charge eight days earlier. Baltusrol isn't a municipal course: it's a private, invitation-only club that has hosted seven U.S. Opens and two PGA Championships, and current reporting puts its initiation fee near **$150,000** with roughly **$18,500** in annual dues ([PrivateIQ](https://www.privateiq.golf/clubs/new-jersey/springfield/baltusrol-golf-club-springfield-nj/membership-cost)) — a venue that is, by design, inaccessible to the overwhelming majority of TX-11 voters. Across 11 Baltusrol charges the campaign's total comes to **$16,988.10**.

Elsewhere: **$37,819.04** to the **St. Joe Resort** in Panama City Beach, Florida for "facility rental & catering" on 2025-10-15 (`C00753913/schedule_b:337`); **$11,200.00** and **$8,371.09** to a Hilton in DC (`C00719294/schedule_b:2369`, `:2337`); repeat stays at the **Post Oak Hotel** (Houston's only Forbes Five-Star hotel) and the **Omni Dallas Convention Center**; and smaller but repeated charges at the **JW Marriott Essex House** overlooking Central Park in New York. Most of these are booked as "lodging," "facility rental," or "catering" — the unglamorous accounting labels underneath what are, functionally, donor retreats at some of the country's most exclusive addresses. The pattern continues into the most recent, not-yet-processed filings too: an Odessa Marriott charge on 2026-05-07 (`C00719294/efile:510`), a Hilton and a JW Marriott Houston charge the same week (`C00719294/efile:579`, `:799`), all pulled from the raw efile rather than the processed schedule_b export.

## Getting there in style

None of this is much good if you have to drive yourself, and the committees spent **$48,703.48** across 35 charges making sure nobody had to. The biggest chunk, **$29,086.00** across just 4 charges, went to **BLS Limo Group**, including a single **$17,286.00** "transportation" charge from the Pfluger Victory Committee on 2025-02-13 (`C00753913/schedule_b:331`) and two $5,555.00 "charter bus rental" charges six days apart in June 2025 (`C00719294/schedule_b:2313`, `:2314`).

**Savoya**, a national black-car and chauffeur service marketed to corporate and political clients, was paid **$12,471.99** across 13 charges, mostly out of Dallas (`C00719294/schedule_b:2265`, `:2130`, `:2113`, and others). Smaller line items round out the picture: **Carey Limousine**, **Dallas Taxi and Limo** (repeated small charges, evidently a standing account), **MK Black Car Service**, **Orlando Black Car**, and **Snow Country Limousine** (Park City again — the ski-trip transportation).

## Trinkets for the donor class

Not every luxury dollar bought a meal or a room. Campaigns routinely buy small gifts for supporters and staff, and Pfluger's committees spent roughly **$121,600** on items filed as "SUPPORTER GIFTS" — officially bucketed by the FEC as "Campaign Materials," which also covers yard signs and bumper stickers, but the actual vendor list here skews upmarket. The single biggest identifiable gift-adjacent vendor is **Macy's** (the Fair Oaks, Virginia location), billed **$10,541.70** across 10 separate charges (`C00719294/schedule_b:2192`, `:2193`, `:1976`, and others) — including two identical **$2,432.70** charges on the same day, 2025-01-07 (lines 2192 and 2193), and two identical **$648.72** charges the next day. **M.L. Leddy's**, a legendary San Angelo boutique specializing in handmade Western boots and saddlery that has outfitted celebrities and presidents, took **$3,470.00** (`C00749481/schedule_b:437`) plus a further $286.86 (`C00719294/schedule_b:1403`). Holland Jewelry, Inc. billed $378.88 for a supporter gift; Lucchese Bootmaker (another storied Texas boot brand) billed $259.80; and Chouquette Chocolates, a boutique DC chocolatier, billed $763.20 twice.

The most recent gift purchases — again, from the raw efile, still absent from the processed export — trend upmarket too: **Criquet Shirts**, an Austin-based premium polo brand, billed **$5,170.68** in a single charge on 2026-04-17 (`C00753913/efile-2026-07-19T17_28_08.csv:115`); **Tuckernuck**, a DC-based women's boutique known for prep-style clothing, billed **$2,883.20** and **$149.05** in May and June 2026 (`C00753913/efile:111`, `:108`); and **Apis World**, a Frisco, Texas gift vendor, billed $900.00 on 2026-05-06 (`C00753913/efile:110`).

Some of this is genuinely modest — the House Gift Shop runs (as low as $5.05), custom-framed photos from FrameTech, floral arrangements, and a steady stream of $100–$300 charges to **Man Cave Custom Gifts**, a San Angelo shop, read as ordinary retail-politics thank-you items, not extravagance. But the presence of a department-store account, boutique bootmakers, and a chocolatier on the same ledger as Capital Grille and Stein Eriksen Lodge fits the same throughline: a campaign whose retail spending, like its dining and lodging, skews toward the tastes of the donor class it's trying to keep happy.

## The math, laid plain

Add it up and the four categories above — fine dining ($264,658.12), retreats and resorts ($242,557.90), donor gifts (~$121,600), and luxury ground transport ($48,703.48) — come to roughly **$678,000**, against roughly **$8.1 million** in total disbursements across the three committees once the most recent, not-yet-processed filings are folded into both sides of that comparison (see Methodology). That's not the biggest line on the ledger; transfers between Pfluger's own committees and to the National Republican Congressional Committee (NRCC) dwarf it, as does ordinary payroll and digital-ad spending. But at roughly **8% of everything spent** — call it a dollar in every twelve — it's a big enough slice that it isn't rounding error, and it's concentrated almost entirely in one function: **raising more money from people who already have a lot of it.**

## What this actually says

1. **The fundraising apparatus is the point, not a side effect.** Most of the dining and lodging charges above are filed by the campaign itself as "Solicitation and Fundraising" or "Campaign Event" expenses — not staff meals, not constituent outreach. The steakhouses and ski lodges *are* the fundraising operation.

2. **The venues select for a donor, not a voter.** A $150,000-initiation golf club, a five-star ski lodge, a Republicans-only private club two blocks from the Capitol — these aren't places an average TX-11 resident in Abilene or San Angelo will ever set foot in. They're built to be comfortable for exactly the kind of donor Pfluger's top contributor list shows: energy-industry executives and the PACs that represent them.

3. **It's a closed loop.** Oil and gas money — Permian Basin operators, Koch, Valero, API — funds a campaign that spends a meaningful share of it wining and dining more of the same donor class at venues most of those donors would recognize from their own personal lives. The transaction isn't really "candidate meets voter"; it's "campaign apparatus meets checkbook," repeated at Capital Grille tables and Deer Valley lodges instead of union halls or diners.

4. **This is normal, not exceptional, campaign finance practice** — and that's arguably the more unsettling point. Nothing catalogued here appears to violate FEC rules; this is how modern congressional fundraising works across both parties. The "oligarchy" isn't a conspiracy in the shadows — it's an itemized, publicly filed, perfectly legal spreadsheet.

## A few honest caveats

This is legal campaign spending, filed as required, and none of it is evidence of personal enrichment — FEC rules generally prohibit converting campaign funds to personal use, and the categories above (facility rental, catering, transportation, campaign materials) are the normal accounting language for donor-facing campaign events, not a scandal by themselves. The keyword-matching approach used to build this report (see Methodology below) can both over- and under-catch: a search for "grille" or "bistro" could in principle sweep in an unrelated vendor, and differently spelled or unlisted venues won't show up at all. One such over-catch was found and corrected in this report: an automated match on "man cave" pulled in $20,276.00 of printing charges to a San Antonio print shop that is a different business from the gift shop of a similar name — excluded from the donor-gifts total above. Every dollar figure and vendor name above has been checked against the source spreadsheet at the cited file and line number — readers are encouraged to open those files themselves and check our work.
