# TX-11: Claire Reynolds — Financial Disclosure Summary (2026 Cycle)

Claire Reynolds's 2026-cycle campaign, run through a single committee (**Claire Reynolds for Congress**, C00929711), has raised **$65,455.74** in itemized donor receipts and spent **$37,885.88**. This is a small, early-stage operation compared to an entrenched incumbent's — but the shape of the money is telling: this is a grassroots, small-dollar, out-of-district-leaning campaign, not one built on major local or industry donors.

A critical note on timing: more than half of everything reported below — **$34,604.04** of the $65,455.74 in receipts, and **$10,437.74** of the $37,885.88 in disbursements — comes from the campaign's second quarter (April–June 2026), which was *not yet visible* in the FEC's own "processed" bulk export at the time this report was generated. That data only shows up because this analysis specifically goes looking for it in the campaign's raw electronic filings. See the Methodology section for exactly what that means and why it matters.

The FEC's own spending-category coding is very sparse for this committee — all but two of the committee's disbursement rows come back with no `category_code_full` value at all, which is common for smaller, newer campaigns that haven't been through a full FEC audit cycle. This is a campaign whose spending is straightforwardly visible in the data, without the vendor-obscuring patterns bigger operations sometimes show.

## Takeaways

1. **A full quarter of activity was nearly invisible, and the size of it matters.** More than half this cycle's fundraising ($34,604.04 of $65,455.74) and disbursements ($10,437.74 of $37,885.88) happened in Q2 2026 (April–June) — a period the FEC's processed bulk export hadn't caught up to as of this report. Anyone pulling only the standard schedule_a/schedule_b files, without checking the campaign's raw efile submissions, would see a campaign that looked like it had gone quiet after Q1, when in fact its second quarter outraised its first.

2. **This is a small-dollar, digitally-native operation, not a locally-funded one.** No PACs beyond a single $5,000 national digital-fundraising check, no industry cluster, no self-evidently wealthy local patron — the money comes from individual donors giving in the thousands, not tens of thousands, split between in-district Texans and out-of-state progressive donors reached through national networks like Vote Save America.

3. **Spending is almost entirely organizing infrastructure, not persuasion media.** There's no line item here for television, radio, or a paid consulting shop taking a six- or seven-figure cut — the biggest checks go to mail, yard signs, ballot access, and the CRM/fundraising software stack (ActBlue, NGP VAN, Bonterra/EveryAction) that underlies basically every modern campaign's operations. That's consistent with a campaign still building its ground game rather than one already running a paid persuasion campaign.

4. **Vendor-name inconsistency (not double-counting) is a real, if minor, data-cleanliness issue.** The same vendor appears under multiple casings/punctuation in the raw data — "ActBlue"/"ACTBLUE," "PrintMail Pro"/"PRINTMAIL PRO," "Bonterra - EveryAction"/"BONTERRA - EVERYACTION" — which happens because processed-export rows render names in all caps while the campaign's raw efile submissions (used to fill the Q2 gap) preserve normal casing. `analyze-candidate.rb` case-normalizes donor names for exactly this reason, but payee names for disbursements are not deduped the same way, so the payee totals above understate true per-vendor spending slightly unless manually combined (as done above for PrintMail Pro).

5. **The household connection is visible, and appropriately disclosed.** A $972.71 disbursement to "BEARDSLEY, TOD" and reimbursements to the candidate herself both appear in the itemized data — normal, disclosed campaign-finance activity involving the candidate's spouse and the candidate's own reimbursements, not a red flag, but worth naming plainly given this candidate's household is also documented in this repo's House Ethics data (see Methodology). That House Ethics filing also shows Reynolds herself earned a $120,000 salary from the State Bar of Texas the year before filing — a working attorney's income, not independent wealth, funding a campaign that (per the donor and spending data above) is otherwise running on small grassroots contributions rather than a self-funded war chest.
