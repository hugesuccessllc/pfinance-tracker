# TX-31: Justin Early — Financial Disclosure Summary

Justin Early is running for Texas's 31st Congressional District, a Williamson County-anchored seat (Round Rock, Georgetown, Killeen, Fort Cavazos) held since 2003 by Republican incumbent John Carter. One FEC committee reports activity for Early: his principal campaign committee, **Justin Early for Congress** (C00907329), covering roughly June 2025 through March 2026. The committee reported **$30,389.00** in itemized outside contributions, plus **$9,842.10** in in-kind contributions and **$32,800.00** in loans from the candidate himself — a combined funding base of roughly **$73,031**. Against that, the committee reported **$80,011.15** in itemized disbursements. This is a modest, early-stage challenger operation: no House Ethics Committee financial disclosure filing exists for Early in the data reviewed (see Takeaway 4), and several funding and spending patterns below point to a campaign still building basic infrastructure rather than running paid voter contact at scale.

Itemized outside contributions — individuals and political committees, excluding the candidate's own money — total $30,389.00: $27,639.00 from individuals and $2,750.00 from political committees. The largest donors:

9. **John Crisp** — $1,000.00 — not employed (Georgetown, TX)
10. **Sean Richards** — $1,000.00 — Sales, Swimlane (Dallas, TX)

The top donor, Wilson Early, shares the candidate's surname and lives in Leander — a few miles from Early's own city of Georgetown — strongly suggesting a family contribution rather than an unrelated donor coincidence. Beyond that, the donor list is small-dollar and Texas-heavy: individuals in Texas account for $23,764.00 of the $27,639.00 raised from individuals (86%, across 73 contributions), with the remainder scattered across Maryland, California, Louisiana, and a handful of other states in amounts under $500 apiece. The one PAC gift of note, $2,500.00 from the Machinists Non-Partisan Political League, is a national labor union PAC. Every itemized individual contribution was processed through ActBlue, the Democratic Party's online donation platform — consistent with the $1,000.00 contribution the campaign later made to the Texas Democratic Party (see Major Spending) in pointing to this being a Democratic campaign, though FEC data doesn't carry a direct party-affiliation field to confirm it outright.

Notably absent from this list: any corporate PAC money, and any contribution near the federal individual maximum ($3,500 per election). This reads as an early-stage grassroots list rather than one built around max-out donors or institutional backing.

## Major Spending

Itemized disbursements break down heavily toward back-office and vendor overhead rather than voter-facing advertising. **Administrative/Salary/Overhead Expenses** is the largest category by far at $46,494.31 (58% of spending, 84 transactions), followed by **Campaign Materials** at $13,110.20, an **Uncategorized** bucket at $9,842.10 (explained below), **Advertising Expenses** at $6,116.18, **Campaign Event Expenses** at $3,000.00, a $1,000.00 **Political Contribution**, and $898.36 in **Solicitation and Fundraising Expenses**.

The single largest vendor is **1926 Prints** (Waco, TX) at $17,661.58 across several payments described as "campaign website and general consulting" — including a $10,000.00 payment on July 2, 2025 and a $5,000.00 payment on July 30, 2025, both in the campaign's first two months. **American Printing and Mailing** (Austin, TX) follows at $13,110.20 for physical printing. Two out-of-state consulting firms round out the top vendors: **Sunrise Political Solutions** (La Mesa, CA), $7,261.95 for compliance services, and **Johnson Smith Consulting LLC** (Lexington, KY), $6,000.00 in direct consulting-fee payments — plus an additional $2,500.00 routed through an in-kind reimbursement (see below), for $8,500.00 total to that one Kentucky-based firm. **Polaris Campaigns Inc** (Chicago, IL) received $5,000.00 for digital advertising, and **EveryAction Inc** (Washington, DC) received $3,223.32 for fundraising software.

The $9,842.10 "Uncategorized" line, and the $9,842.10 attributed to payee "Early, Justin" in the underlying data, is not a payment to the candidate personally — it's the FEC's standard double-entry treatment of in-kind contributions. Ten times between June and September 2025, Early paid for a campaign expense (event venue rentals, a fundraising consultant invoice, web services, printing, speech coaching, software) out of his own pocket and donated the value to the campaign; FEC rules require that value to be booked both as a receipt (Schedule A, "Contributions From the Candidate") and as a matching operating expenditure (Schedule B), so it appears twice in the raw data by design. Digging into the memo detail behind these entries reveals the actual vendors: the largest, a $6,249.43 "event venue rental," was paid to **The Vinyard at Florence**, a Texas event venue; a $2,500.00 "fundraising consultant invoice" went to Johnson Smith Consulting LLC (the same firm noted above); and smaller amounts went to DKR Strategies, LLC and Peerspace (a venue/workspace booking platform).

Two performance-fee payments to **Trey Taylor** (Broomfield, CO) in October 2025 are worth a note on how to read this data correctly: the raw transaction list shows two separate $3,000.00 "PERFORMANCE FEE" charges, which would suggest $6,000.00 spent on an event performer. A same-day $3,000.00 correction (negative-amount) row tied to one of those charges means the real, net cost to the campaign was $3,000.00, not $6,000.00 — a reminder that any individual-transaction list needs to be read alongside category and payee totals, which already net corrections out correctly.

Finally, the campaign made one outbound political contribution: $1,000.00 to the **Texas Democratic Party** in July 2025, an early show of institutional alignment consistent with the ActBlue-heavy donor base above.

## Takeaways

1. **The candidate is self-funding more than his outside donors combined.** Between $32,800.00 in personal loans and $9,842.10 in in-kind contributions, Early has put roughly $42,642 of his own money and value into the campaign — more than the $30,389.00 raised in itemized outside contributions. For a challenger this early in a cycle, that's a meaningful signal about how much financial risk the candidate himself is carrying, and it's debt (the loans, at least) that would need to be repaid from future fundraising.

2. **The top donor appears to be family.** Wilson Early of Leander, TX — a few miles from the candidate's own home base of Georgetown — gave the single largest itemized contribution, $3,500.00, and lists no employer. Family seed money at the top of a donor list is common for first-time challengers, but it underscores how early-stage this campaign's fundraising still is relative to a fully donor-driven operation.

3. **Spending skews toward setting up infrastructure, not reaching voters.** Fifty-eight percent of itemized spending ($46,494.31) falls under Administrative/Salary/Overhead, led by a single Waco firm's $17,661.58 for "website and general consulting" — more than double what the campaign spent on Advertising ($6,116.18). That's a normal pattern for a campaign's first several months, but it means the $80,011.15 spent so far has bought comparatively little direct voter contact.

4. **No House Ethics Committee financial disclosure is on file for this candidate.** Unlike sitting House members, who file annual Financial Disclosure Statements and Periodic Transaction Reports with the Committee on Ethics, no such filing exists in the data collected for Early — and federal law requires any candidate who raises or spends more than $5,000, which this campaign has, to file one. That threshold for filing (typically 30 days after crossing $5,000, or by the annual May 15 deadline) means this could reflect a filing that's simply not yet due, a pending extension, or an actual compliance gap — it's worth checking directly against the House Clerk's public disclosure portal rather than assuming either explanation.

5. **The donor base and one outbound contribution both point toward a Democratic campaign, without FEC data stating it outright.** Every itemized individual contribution ran through ActBlue, the campaign gave $1,000.00 to the Texas Democratic Party, and its one PAC contribution came from a labor union (the Machinists). None of that is a formal party-affiliation field, but taken together it's a consistent, unambiguous pattern in a race where the sitting incumbent, John Carter, is a longtime Republican.

- Applying these checks didn't change the topline receipt/disbursement totals from a naive `SUM()` (there were no memo-row or sign-filtering errors to correct in the aggregate figures), but it did change how two individual findings are described: the Trey Taylor "largest disbursements" entry (net $3,000, not $6,000) and the "Early, Justin" payee line (an in-kind accounting artifact, not a personal payment).
