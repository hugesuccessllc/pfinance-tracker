# TX-37: Greg Casar — Financial Disclosure Summary

Greg Casar is the Democratic incumbent running in Texas's newly numbered 37th Congressional District (the redrawn Austin-area seat he currently holds as TX-35). Three FEC committees report activity in this filing period: his principal campaign committee, **Greg Casar for Congress** (C00791897); a leadership PAC, **Our Fight Our Future** (C00811216); and a joint fundraising committee, **Casar Victory Fund** (C00918219). Combined, the three reported **$826,173.30** in itemized receipts and **$773,117.47** in itemized disbursements for the period covering roughly January 2025 through June 2026. The campaign committee alone ended the period with **$597,005.39** cash on hand against $10,000 in committee debt; the leadership PAC and joint fundraising committee are much smaller, ending with $5,799.50 and $1,739.50 on hand, respectively.

Itemized donations — individuals and political committees only, excluding internal transfers — total $774,673.30 to the campaign committee, $12,500.00 to Our Fight Our Future, and $39,000.00 to Casar Victory Fund. Across all three, individuals gave roughly $586,000 and PACs/committees gave roughly $240,000. The largest donors:

3. **Machinists Non-Partisan Political League** — $10,000.00 — Upper Marlboro, MD
4. **American Federation of State, County & Municipal Employees (AFSCME) PEOPLE** — $10,000.00 to the campaign, plus an additional $5,000.00 to Our Fight Our Future ($15,000 total)
5. **Vaughn Venerberg** — $8,500.00 — not employed (Dallas, TX), via Casar Victory Fund
6. **Elizabeth Simons** — $7,000.00 — Chair, Heising-Simons Foundation (Atherton, CA)

Just outside the top 10: **William Miller**, a lobbyist at Hillco Partners — Texas's largest state-level lobbying firm — gave $6,800.00, and **Domingo Garcia**, a Dallas civil-rights attorney and former national LULAC president, gave $6,000.00.

Beyond individuals, the PAC donor list is dominated by organized labor: the Machinists' and AFSCME's PACs (above) are joined by the Air Line Pilots Association PAC ($6,000), the Engineers Political Education Committee ($5,000), the Teamsters' D.R.I.V.E. fund ($5,000), and the NEA Advocacy Fund ($5,000). Corporate PAC money is essentially absent from the itemized data.

Geographically, Texas donors lead ($354,737.76 across 1,122 contributions), but Washington, DC ($156,850.00, 79 contributions — almost entirely labor and issue PACs) and California ($99,211.50, 175 contributions) are both substantial, followed by Massachusetts, New York, Maryland, Virginia, and Illinois. For a district-based House campaign, this is a notably national donor footprint — more so than a typical challenger or freshman would show, and consistent with Casar's profile as a national figure within the progressive movement.

## Major Spending

Itemized disbursements break down into a few clear buckets. Digital advertising is the single largest line: **$140,363.84** to Aisle 518, a Washington, DC digital ad and list-rental firm, spread across 13 payments (including one $40,000 and one $15,000 buy). Payroll and payroll-adjacent costs come next — **$87,187.05** in direct payroll (182 transactions) plus **$25,559.50** in payroll taxes, largely run through Gusto ($29,595.20 total) and paid to named staffers directly (Colin Diersing $18,286.96, Sawyer Hackett $16,000.00, Stephanie Trinh $14,585.70, Cynthia Jasso-Rotunno $11,333.85, Claire White $8,704.06, Irene Chavez-Galvan $7,320.75).

Fundraising overhead is the next-largest category: **$71,260.89** to HM Consulting (Washington, DC) for "fundraising services," billed in roughly $12,000 installments about every two months, plus separate campaign-management retainers to Laura Hernandez Consulting ($37,500.00) and JTX Strategies ($22,750.00, both Austin-based). Software and data cost $40,639.61 (mostly NGP VAN, the standard Democratic campaign CRM), and PCMS billed $30,844.79 for recurring monthly accounting services. Card-processing and transaction fees (ActBlue and American Express) add another $26,912.75.

The committees also gave money outward. Beyond the campaign's own $80,000 in "Other Disbursements" — which includes contributions to Sylvia Garcia for Congress, Maxwell Alejandro Frost for Congress, several other Democratic House candidates in other states, the Travis County Democratic Party ($10,500 combined), and a spread of Austin-area groups (Austinites for Equality, Black Austin Democrats, PODER, Dove Springs Proud) — the leadership PAC, Our Fight Our Future, separately gave $25,000 across 17 contributions to federal candidates and committees, including $5,000 to the Congressional Progressive Caucus PAC and $2,500 to CHC BOLD PAC (the Congressional Hispanic Caucus's PAC), plus $17,500 spread across a dozen individual Democratic congressional candidates nationally (including two separate contributions to Josh Riley for Congress) and two state/local party and PAC entities.

One entry deserves a closer look. The campaign's largest single disbursement is a $50,000 payment to "AMERICAN EXPRESS," described only as "CREDIT CARD PAYMENT, SEE BELOW." Digging into the memo sub-transactions that back-reference it — the same mechanism campaigns use to itemize routine card purchases (restaurants, hotels, airlines) behind a lump card payment — reveals that this particular $50,000 line isn't retail spending at all: it's a single $50,000 "UNLIMITED TRANSFER TO PARTY COMMITTEE" to the DCCC, apparently processed through the campaign's Amex account rather than reported as a standalone transfer. The other 14 lump card payments (totaling $35,124.89) are ordinary travel, catering, and hotel itemizations — Delta, United, Enterprise, Marriott, and similar vendors — nothing unusual there.

Separately, $36,050.00 moved between the committees themselves: Casar Victory Fund, the joint fundraising vehicle, redistributed its proceeds to the campaign ($22,550.00) and to Our Fight Our Future ($13,600.00). That's bookkeeping, not spending, and is reported here only for completeness.

## Takeaways

1. **Casar runs a leadership PAC that bankrolls the national progressive movement, not just his own race.** Our Fight Our Future's $25,000 in outbound contributions to the Congressional Progressive Caucus PAC, CHC BOLD PAC, and about a dozen individual Democratic candidates across multiple states — combined with the campaign committee's own $30,000 in direct contributions to other candidates and local Texas organizations (separate from the $50,000 DCCC transfer discussed above) — paints a picture of a member actively building coalition and influence beyond a single, safely blue Austin seat.

2. **A $50,000 "credit card payment" was actually a party transfer.** The campaign's largest disbursement was booked as an American Express payment, but the underlying memo detail shows it was a lump $50,000 transfer to the DCCC. Nothing in the topline numbers is wrong, but a reader relying only on the "largest payee" table would misread this as retail card spending rather than a direct contribution to the national party's House campaign arm.

3. **The donor base is nationally networked and labor-heavy, not locally retail.** Washington, DC and California together account for more itemized money than most House campaigns raise nationwide, and the PAC contributor list is almost entirely organized labor (Machinists, AFSCME, pilots, engineers, Teamsters, NEA) with no corporate PAC money in evidence. That's consistent with a high national profile, but it also means the donor base looks less like a typical constituent-funded campaign and more like a movement-backed one.

4. **Fundraising and consulting overhead is a meaningfully large line item.** Between HM Consulting's fundraising retainer ($71,260.89), campaign-management contracts ($60,250.00 combined), and standard card-processing fees, professional services and fundraising overhead run well into six figures — a normal cost of a well-funded campaign, but worth noting alongside the direct voter-facing spend (digital ads, texting, printing).

5. **No personal financial disclosure content is available yet this cycle.** The only House Ethics filing on record for Casar is a request for a 90-day extension on his 2025 annual Financial Disclosure, pushing the due date to August 13, 2026. That filing also still lists his current district as TX-35, the seat he holds today — a reminder that the TX-37 numbering used throughout this filing period reflects the district he's running for under the new map, not the one his personal disclosures are currently filed under. No asset, income, or stock-trade detail exists in the data pulled for this analysis.

## Methodology & AI Transparency

**Model:** Claude Sonnet 5 (`claude-sonnet-5`), via Claude Code (VS Code extension). No user-configurable temperature or token-limit overrides were set for this session; the analysis ran under Claude Code's standard default sampling and context settings.

**Data-integrity notes specific to this run:**

- The three committee directories (`C00791897`, `C00811216`, `C00918219`) were already correctly named by committee ID, avoiding the candidate-ID naming pitfall documented from a prior run.
- Standard checks from the tool's header comments were run: no duplicate `transaction_id` values were found in any Schedule A or B file across the three committees, and every `amendment_indicator` value was `A` ("ADD") — no corrections or amendments to net out.
- **This run found and fixed a real bug in `analyze-candidate.rb`'s card-breakdown feature**, which previously identified lump/bulk card payments (Schedule B gotcha #1 in the tool's header) by matching the literal text "SEE MEMO ITEMS" in `disbursement_description`. Greg Casar's committees use a different phrase — "CREDIT CARD PAYMENT, SEE BELOW" — for the identical reporting structure (a lump non-memo payment with `memo_code=X` children back-referencing it), so the text-match approach silently found zero lump payments for this candidate on the first run. The fix identifies parent rows structurally, by the back-reference relationship itself, rather than by description text. Re-running the fixed tool against August Pfluger's (TX-11) existing data as a regression check showed the old approach had *also* been undercounting his lump payments (120 found vs. 155 with the fix, missing about $18,800) — so this wasn't a Casar-only edge case. The fix and this note are recorded together in the tool's header comments per this prompt's standing instruction to document integrity fixes there rather than as a separate correction section here.
- Applying that fix is what surfaced the $50,000 DCCC transfer described in Major Spending above — under the old text-matching logic, that transaction would have shown up only as an opaque $50,000 "AMERICAN EXPRESS" payment with no indication of where the money actually went.
- The `category_code_full` field (FEC's standard spending-category code) is blank for every disbursement row in all three of Casar's committees, unlike Pfluger's data where it was populated. This appears to be a gap in how this particular export was generated rather than a parsing error — the raw CSVs simply don't carry that field for this candidate. Spending categorization above instead uses the `line_number_label` and `disbursement_description` fields directly, which were populated and consistent.
- Two House Ethics Committee filings exist under `house-ethics/`, but only one PDF (Filing ID #30027195) was present in this candidate's directory, and it is a 90-day filing-extension request rather than a substantive disclosure — see Takeaway 5.
