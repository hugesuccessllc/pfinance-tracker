# TX-37: Lauren Peña — Financial Disclosure Summary

Lauren Peña is the Republican nominee in Texas's redrawn 37th Congressional District, an Austin-area seat currently held by Democratic incumbent Greg Casar. She won a March 3, 2026 primary and a May 26, 2026 runoff against Ge'Nell Gary to earn the nomination, and now faces Casar (and independent James Kinney) in the November general election. One FEC committee reports activity for her candidacy: her principal campaign committee, **LAUREN4TEXAS** (C00906149). Over the roughly 14 months covered by this filing period, it reported **$18,144.50** in itemized receipts and **$115,396.04** in itemized disbursements, ending with just **$769.92** cash on hand and no debt.

That gap between itemized receipts and total spending isn't an error — it reflects how small this campaign's itemized footprint is relative to its real fundraising. Adding the ending cash balance back to total spending puts the campaign's total money raised over its life at roughly **$116,166**, of which only about 16% ($18,144.50) came from donors whose cumulative giving crossed the $200 threshold that triggers FEC itemization. The other roughly 84% came in as unitemized, below-threshold, mostly online small-dollar gifts — a genuinely grassroots-shaped donor base, at least in dollar terms.

## Key Donors

Every itemized dollar in this dataset came from individuals — the data shows $0.00 in PAC or political-committee money, a real distinction from a typical House campaign at this stage, which usually has at least some party or leadership-PAC support layered in. The top itemized donors:

A retiree giving the maximum allowable amount on an automatic monthly cadence is an unusually disciplined, sustained donor relationship for a challenger campaign.

3. **Destiny Garcia** — $1,044.54 — training manager, Jay Henges Center for Youth Leadership (Arlington, VA) — reported as an in-kind airfare contribution, offset by an equal disbursement (see below)
4. **Edward Haussermann** — $999.36 — retired (Franklin, TN)
5. **Carly Tomaine** — $637.00 — director, Jay Henges Center for Youth Leadership (Arlington, VA) — also in-kind airfare
6. **Payton Kieidon** — $592.36 — "Christian leaders program," Leadership Institute (Arlington, VA) — also in-kind airfare

Retirees make up a disproportionate share of the itemized top 10 (four of ten), and the Leadership Institute / Jay Henges Center for Youth Leadership connection — a well-known conservative youth-training nonprofit based in Arlington, Virginia — shows up three times among the top donors, all as in-kind travel rather than cash. There's no evidence of PAC, corporate, or party-committee money anywhere in the itemized data; every donor row is coded as an individual.

## Major Spending

Disbursements split into four FEC categories, and one vendor dominates the total:

- **Solicitation and Fundraising Expenses — $90,634.89** (78.5% of spending, 415 transactions)
- **Administrative/Salary/Overhead Expenses — $15,485.00** (13.4%, 9 transactions)
- **Advertising Expenses — $7,002.25** (6.1%, 5 transactions)
- **Travel Expenses — $2,273.90** (2.0%, 3 transactions)

The single largest payee by far is **Frontline Strategies, LLC.** (Elgin, IL), which received **$82,672.99** across **171 separate payments**, all coded "FUNDRAISING FEE." Frontline Strategies is a real, established Republican digital-fundraising and list-brokerage firm — public reporting puts its 2024-cycle receipts from federal committees above $16 million — not a fringe or one-off vendor. The payment pattern (171 small, frequent charges rather than a handful of flat retainers) is consistent with a commission taken directly out of each donation batch, the standard structure for this kind of prospecting-driven small-dollar fundraising: the vendor is paid to acquire and mail/text donor lists, and gets a cut of what comes back.

The second-largest payee, **I77 Strategies, LLC.** (Roanoke, VA), received $19,862.25 across advertising (texting, $2,041–$2,253 charges), general campaign consulting, and travel reimbursement. **WinRed**, the Republican donation-processing platform, took $4,385.33 in transaction fees. The **Texas Secretary of State** collected a one-time $3,125.00 candidate filing fee, and **i360** — the Koch-network-linked voter-data and targeting firm used widely by GOP campaigns — was paid $2,500.00 for advertising/data services.

One lump payment shows up in the card-breakdown detail: a $1,860.00 charge to I77 Strategies is fully itemized into two underlying items — $1,500.00 for "general campaign consulting and travel reimbursement" and $360.00 to the Texas GOP Convention, presumably a delegate or attendee registration fee.

Three payments to individuals — Destiny Garcia ($1,044.54), Carly Tomaine ($637.00), and Payton Kieidon ($592.36) — are exact dollar-for-dollar matches to their in-kind airfare contributions listed above. This isn't double-counted spending or a circular payment; it's the standard FEC accounting for in-kind gifts, where a supporter who pays for their own travel to help the campaign gets it logged as both a receipt and an offsetting disbursement of identical value, netting to zero in cash terms.

## Takeaways

1. **Roughly 78 cents of every dollar this campaign has raised has gone to a single fundraising vendor.** Frontline Strategies' $82,673 take is 78.5% of total itemized disbursements and about 71% of the campaign's entire estimated lifetime receipts. That's a strikingly high overhead ratio for money that's supposed to fund a general-election campaign against a well-funded incumbent — though it's also a recognizable, if aggressive, version of the donor-list-acquisition model many first-time small-dollar campaigns lean on early, betting that list-building now pays off in the general.

2. **This is a small-dollar-shaped campaign, almost by definition.** Only about 16% of an estimated $116,000 raised was itemized — meaning the overwhelming majority came from donors giving small enough, infrequent enough amounts to stay under the $200 FEC reporting threshold. Combined with $0 in PAC money, the funding profile reads as genuinely grassroots, at least in composition, even as the overhead built into acquiring those small donors ran extremely high.

3. **Her personal financial disclosure describes real financial strain, including housing assistance.** The House Ethics Committee filing (10075544, a Candidate Report covering January 2025–April 2026) lists $50,001–$100,000 in Department of Education student loan debt and a $10,000–$15,000 car loan through Bridgecrest Auto, a subprime auto lender. Schedule F ("Agreements") also discloses a 2026 agreement with the **Housing Authority of the City of Austin** for **Section 8** housing assistance — indicating the candidate herself is a recipient of federal rental assistance. That's an unusually direct, personal financial profile to see in a congressional candidate's disclosure, and stands in sharp contrast to the wealth typically reported by members of Congress; it also lines up with her public biography as a survivor of trafficking and mother of six.

4. **The campaign is essentially spent down heading into the general election.** Ending cash on hand was $769.92 against zero debt — she isn't carrying a deficit, but she also has almost nothing left in the account. For comparison, Greg Casar's campaign committee in this same district (see `tx-37/greg-casar/`) reported $597,005.39 cash on hand over a comparable period — a roughly 775-to-1 cash disparity between the two general-election contenders in the same race.

5. **Her donor and vendor network skews heavily toward out-of-state conservative infrastructure rather than Central Texas.** Beyond Frontline Strategies (Illinois) and I77 Strategies (Virginia), several top donors are tied to the Leadership Institute and its Jay Henges Center for Youth Leadership — both based in Arlington, Virginia — rather than Austin-area supporters. For a district-based House campaign, that's a national-conservative-movement donor and vendor footprint layered on top of a genuinely small-dollar, in-district contribution base.

## Methodology & AI Transparency

**Model:** Claude Sonnet 5 (`claude-sonnet-5`), via Claude Code (VS Code extension). No user-configurable temperature or token-limit overrides were set for this session; the analysis ran under Claude Code's standard default sampling and context settings.

**Data-integrity notes specific to this run:**

- The committee directory (`C00906149`) was already correctly named by committee ID. Only one committee exists for this candidate, and each schedule has a single source CSV, so there was no risk of double-counting across amended or re-exported files.
- Six Schedule A rows carry small negative amounts (Bill Hooten x3, Wayne Swetnam, James Shoemaker, Wayne Lewis — net −$83.86), representing refunds or reattributions rather than noise. Per the tool's documented approach, these were netted into each donor's running total rather than dropped; none of the affected donors appear in the top 10, so this had no effect on the Key Donors list.
- No duplicate `transaction_id` values were found in Schedule A or B, and every row's `is_individual` flag confirms the $0.00 PAC/committee-money finding — there is no committee-coded donor row anywhere in the itemized data.
- The card-breakdown feature (which identifies lump card payments by the back-reference relationship between a non-memo parent row and its `memo_code=X` children, not by matching description text — since committees phrase "see memo items" differently, a bug already found and fixed on a prior candidate's data) found exactly one qualifying lump payment here: the $1,860.00 I77 Strategies charge described above, 100% of which was itemized into its two underlying line items.
- The three in-kind travel contributions (Garcia, Tomaine, Kieidon) were checked against both Schedule A and Schedule B directly, confirming matching dates, amounts, and "IN-KIND - AIRFARE" descriptions on both sides before writing them up as a paired receipt/disbursement rather than flagging them as an unexplained coincidence or double payment.
- The House Ethics filing (10075544) is a substantive Candidate Report, not an extension request. The tool's PDF scanner (which greps for dollar-amount lines) correctly surfaced the two liability rows (student loans, car loan), but the Section 8 housing agreement on Schedule F carries no dollar figure and so wasn't caught by that filter — it was found by reading the extracted PDF text directly, and is worth noting as a real gap in what the automated scan alone would have surfaced. Anyone re-running this analysis on a new candidate should read the full PDF text, not just the tool's filtered `asset_lines` output, before concluding a House Ethics filing has nothing notable in it.
- The candidate's party affiliation, primary/runoff status, and public biography (Republican nominee; defeated Ge'Nell Gary in the May 26, 2026 runoff; trafficking survivor and mother of six; Travis County Republican Party background) came from a web search against Ballotpedia and the candidate's own campaign site, since none of the FEC CSV columns populated for this committee carry a party-affiliation field.
- The Frontline Strategies overhead ratio (Takeaway 1) is reported as a factual spending pattern, not an allegation of wrongdoing — public reporting (via OpenSecrets/Transparency USA search results) confirms Frontline Strategies is an established, large-scale Republican fundraising and list-brokerage vendor used across many federal campaigns, not a shell or one-off entity.
